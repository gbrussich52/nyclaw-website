# 5 Signals That Predict a Motivated Seller in NYC

Every real estate investor wants off-market deals. But in NYC, off-market doesn't mean hidden — it means *knowing where to look* before the brokers do.

The best opportunities come from sellers under pressure. Not necessarily desperation, but circumstance: a tax bill that's grown unmanageable, a building full of violations, a tax break that's about to expire. These owners often sell below market — not because they have to, but because they're tired.

Here are the five data signals that predict a motivated seller in NYC, and where to find each one.

## Signal 1: HPD Violations (Active + Open)

The NYC Department of Housing Preservation and Development tracks building violations by property. A building with 10+ open Class B or C violations is a building the owner is struggling to maintain. That's a workload problem — and workload problems become financial problems over time.

Open violations also affect the owner's ability to refinance. Banks don't like lend against properties with active HPD complaints. That financing squeeze creates selling pressure.

**Where to find it**: HPD online data, or [NYRezone.com](https://nyrezone.com) which aggregates violation counts by parcel.

## Signal 2: Tax Lien Sale List (DOF 90-Day List)

New York City publishes a tax lien sale list — properties delinquent on property taxes that the city is considering selling to third-party lien holders. The city releases the list at 90, 60, 30, and 10 days before the annual sale.

The 90-day list in February is your best lead source. Owners on this list are delinquent, aware of the deadline, and often motivated to sell before the lien sale closes off their options.

**Where to find it**: NYC DOF lien sale datasets, or automated via [NYRezone.com](https://nyrezone.com) which flags lien sale candidates on the parcel map.

## Signal 3: ACRIS Lis Pendens (Pre-Foreclosure Filings)

A lis pendens is a legal notice filed in ACRIS (NYC's deed records) when a lender begins foreclosure proceedings. It means the owner has fallen behind on their mortgage — but hasn't lost the property yet.

This is the window. Between lis pendens filing and actual foreclosure, many owners will negotiate. They want to preserve some equity. They're not going to get market rate, but they'll sell.

**Where to find it**: ACRIS property records (requires knowing the BBL), or [NYRezone.com](https://nyrezone.com) which surfaces active lis pendens by parcel.

## Signal 4: 421-a Expiration

421-a was NYC's affordable housing tax exemption that gave developers major property tax breaks in exchange for affordable units. Thousands of buildings built in the 1990s–2000s had 421-a exemptions that have started expiring.

When 421-a expires, the owner's tax bill increases — sometimes dramatically. A building that was cash-flowing with a tax exemption may be cash-flow negative without it. That financial shock creates immediate selling motivation.

**Where to find it**: NYC Finance property records. [NYRezone.com](https://nyrezone.com) flags parcels where 421-a is active and shows the estimated expiry year.

## Signal 5: Long Hold + No Recent Activity

An owner who has held a property for 25+ years, made no capital improvements (no recent DOB permits), and hasn't refinanced recently is often someone who's reached an exit point. Estate planning, retirement, partnership dissolution — these "quiet" motivations are just as powerful as financial distress.

The data signals here: year acquired (from ACRIS), last permit activity (DOB), last mortgage (ACRIS), owner address out-of-state (often absentee).

**Where to find it**: ACRIS + DOB records, or aggregated in [NYRezone.com](https://nyrezone.com).

## Stack the Signals

The most actionable leads have **multiple signals at once**. A parcel with HPD violations + a lien sale flag + 421-a expiring in 12 months is a rare combination — and exactly the kind of situation where an owner will sell at a discount to avoid the compounding problem.
